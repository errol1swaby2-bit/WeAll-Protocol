# WeAll Genesis Constitution — Draft 2

**Version 0.1 — Draft 2 for Genesis Constitutional Review**

Constitutional Direct Democracy • Human Dignity • Due Process • Anti-Domination

## Preamble

WeAll is founded on the belief that social life, civic trust, community decision-making, and public coordination should not be owned by any single corporation, state, faction, class, or hidden authority.

WeAll exists to give people a shared protocol through which verified humans can speak, organize, deliberate, review disputes, form groups, assign responsibility, govern shared resources, and make collective decisions without surrendering their civic presence to a centralized platform.

This Constitution establishes the first principles of the WeAll network. It is the founding public record of the protocol and the civic foundation from which all future governance must proceed.

WeAll is not pure majority rule. WeAll is constitutional direct democracy: the people may govern directly, but no majority may use governance to deny another person's equal humanity, due process, dignity, privacy, voice, or lawful participation under the rules of the protocol.

The network begins with this commitment:

No person shall be reduced beneath another by race, color, ethnicity, nationality, ancestry, sex, gender, gender identity, sexual orientation, age, disability, religion, conscience, family status, poverty, class, housing status, political minority, lawful belief, or any other condition of human identity.

The purpose of WeAll is not domination. The purpose of WeAll is coordination in dignity.

---

## Article I — Founding Status

### Section 1. Genesis Constitutional Record

This Constitution shall be recorded as the Genesis Constitution of the WeAll network.

The Genesis Constitution shall exist in two forms:

1. The Immutable Genesis Declaration, permanently preserved as the first constitutional record of the network.

2. The Active Constitution, which may be amended only according to the amendment procedures established in this Constitution.

The Immutable Genesis Declaration may never be deleted, hidden, overwritten, or replaced. Future amendments may alter the Active Constitution, but the original founding record shall remain permanently visible and auditable.

### Section 2. Constitutional Supremacy

This Constitution is the highest civic rule of the WeAll network.

No proposal, vote, group rule, moderation action, role assignment, treasury action, validator action, node policy, frontend interface, or governance decision may lawfully override this Constitution.

Any protocol action that violates the Constitution is void within the WeAll governance order, even if approved by a temporary majority.

### Section 3. Protocol Authority

The WeAll protocol shall be the source of authority for identity state, verification state, roles, governance records, group membership, dispute outcomes, reputation records, treasury permissions, and other protocol-governed civic state.

No frontend, node operator, relay, helper service, indexer, storage provider, or private interface may become the final civic authority over users.

## Article II — Core Rights of Users

### Section 1. Equal Civic Standing

Every user of WeAll has equal basic civic standing.

Equal civic standing means that no user may be denied basic access to the protections of this Constitution because of identity, background, status, popularity, faction, wealth, technical skill, node choice, or disagreement with a majority.

Verification level, reputation, roles, and responsibilities may affect what actions a user can perform, but they shall not erase a person's basic constitutional rights.

### Section 2. Right to Human Dignity

Every user has the right to be treated as a human being with dignity.

No governance action may classify any person or group as inherently inferior, subhuman, disposable, naturally servile, permanently excluded, or unworthy of equal civic protection.

Conduct may be judged. Harm may be reviewed. Responsibilities may be assigned or removed. But a person's basic humanity may not be voted away.

### Section 3. Right to Voice

Every user has the right to lawful expression within the rules of the protocol.

The network may establish rules against harassment, threats, coercion, targeted abuse, spam, fraud, impersonation, incitement to violence, sexual exploitation, exploitation of minors, privacy invasion, and other serious harms.

But no user may be silenced merely for peaceful disagreement, minority opinion, unpopular belief, criticism of authority, criticism of governance, or refusal to conform to a dominant faction.

### Section 4. Right to Due Process

No user may be banned, demoted, stripped of role authority, reputationally punished, excluded from a group, deprived of governance participation, or otherwise sanctioned by protocol governance without due process.

Due process includes, at minimum:

1. Notice of the claim, report, allegation, or proposed sanction.

2. A meaningful opportunity to respond.

3. Access to relevant public evidence, except where privacy or safety requires limited disclosure.

4. Review by authorized reviewers, jurors, moderators, or governance actors according to protocol rules.

5. A recorded outcome.

6. A path for appeal or reconsideration where the sanction is significant.

Emergency limits may exist for immediate safety, but emergency actions must be reviewable, time-limited, and auditable.

### Section 5. Right to Privacy and Limited Disclosure

Every user has the right to privacy in personal evidence, identity materials, verification materials, sensitive dispute materials, and other information not intentionally made public outside the protocol-native public content model.

Proof-of-Humanity evidence, dispute evidence, and external communications outside protocol scope shall be handled according to privacy-preserving procedures wherever possible.

The protocol should prefer commitments, hashes, scoped reviewer access, consent-based disclosure, and minimal public exposure over unnecessary publication of sensitive material.

### Section 6. Right to Portability

Every user has the right to preserve their civic identity, verification state, records, receipts, and lawful participation across healthy nodes and compatible clients.

No node operator or frontend may claim ownership over a user's civic presence.

A user's account, history, proofs, and rights belong to the user within the rules of the protocol, not to the access point through which the user connects.

### Section 7. Right to Appeal and Correction

Every user has the right to seek correction of false, mistaken, outdated, malicious, or procedurally defective records.

Reputation systems, moderation systems, dispute systems, verification systems, and governance records must include mechanisms for appeal, correction, expiration, context, or review.

No person should be permanently trapped by a single mistake, false accusation, factional attack, or outdated judgment without a meaningful path to restoration.

## Article III — Anti-Domination Principles

### Section 1. Prohibition on Supremacy Governance

No proposal, rule, group charter, moderation policy, role policy, algorithmic rule, treasury condition, or governance action may establish or promote civic supremacy of one race, sex, gender, ethnicity, religion, class, caste, nationality, or identity group over another.

The network shall reject governance that attempts to create permanent domination, subordination, or exclusion based on human identity.

### Section 2. Protection Against Majoritarian Abuse

A majority may govern ordinary matters, but a majority may not use governance to destroy the equal rights of a minority.

No temporary wave of anger, factional campaign, ideological bloc, bot-amplified pressure, or coordinated group may remove the constitutional protections of a person or minority group without meeting the highest constitutional standards.

### Section 3. Conduct-Based Accountability

The network may hold users accountable for conduct, including harmful conduct, coercive conduct, violent threats, harassment, fraud, impersonation, abuse of process, organized manipulation, or attempts to capture the protocol.

Accountability must be based on conduct and evidence, not on inherent identity or collective prejudice.

### Section 4. Protection Against Patriarchal, Racial, and Extremist Capture

No governance action may establish male dominance, racial hierarchy, ethnic exclusion, religious supremacy, political caste, or any comparable form of human domination as a rule of the network.

Groups may form around lawful beliefs, cultures, interests, and identities, but no group may use WeAll governance to deny the equal civic standing of others.

---

## Article IV — Direct Democracy

### Section 1. Democratic Foundation

WeAll is founded as a direct-democracy coordination protocol.

Verified humans may participate directly in governance according to the rules of the protocol.

The right to participate in governance may be conditioned by verification, reputation, group membership, role assignment, jurisdiction, responsibility, or other protocol rules, but such conditions must be transparent, reviewable, and consistent with this Constitution.

### Section 2. One Verified Human, One Civic Presence

The civic foundation of WeAll is the verified human, not capital ownership, inherited status, institutional rank, or algorithmic favoritism.

The protocol shall resist Sybil attacks, duplicate civic identities, impersonation, coercive account control, and other attacks that corrupt human-centered governance.

### Section 3. Limits of Voting

Voting is not the whole of democracy.

Democracy within WeAll includes:

1. Speech.

2. Deliberation.

3. Evidence.

4. Review.

5. Consent.

6. Participation.

7. Responsibility.

8. Appeal.

9. Transparency.

10. Protection of basic rights.

A vote that violates constitutional rights is not democratic merely because it has numerical support.

### Section 4. Locality and Group Self-Governance

Groups may govern their own internal affairs according to their charters, provided that group rules do not violate this Constitution.

Group self-governance may include membership rules, posting rules, moderation procedures, treasury rules, emissary elections, dispute review, and shared-resource decisions.

The protocol shall prefer local decision-making for local matters and network-wide decision-making for network-wide matters.

---

## Article V — Proof of Humanity and Civic Identity

### Section 1. Native Human Verification

WeAll shall maintain native Proof-of-Humanity processes to help establish human civic presence without requiring centralized identity providers as final authority.

Proof-of-Humanity is a civic safeguard against Sybil attacks, spam, impersonation, and capture. It is not a declaration of moral worth.

### Section 2. Verification Tiers

The protocol may recognize different verification levels for different capabilities.

A basic account may exist before full verification. Higher trust actions may require stronger verification.

Verification levels must be transparent, appealable, and governed by protocol rules.

### Section 3. Privacy in Verification

Proof-of-Humanity evidence shall be handled with strict privacy discipline.

The protocol should avoid publishing raw sensitive identity evidence where cryptographic commitments, reviewer-private access, hashes, or other privacy-preserving methods are sufficient.

### Section 4. No Permanent Identity Caste

Verification status may open or restrict certain actions, but no verification tier may create a permanent caste of human worth.

Users must have a fair path to apply, reapply, appeal, recover, or challenge verification outcomes.

---

## Article VI — Roles, Responsibility, and Reputation

### Section 1. Separation of Humanity and Authority

Being verified as human does not automatically make a user a validator, juror, moderator, node operator, treasury signer, emissary, storage provider, or governance executor.

Service authority must come from explicit role assignment, election, reputation, eligibility, protocol state, or other constitutional process.

### Section 2. Responsibility-Based Roles

Roles within WeAll are responsibilities, not titles of superiority.

Users who accept roles must act according to the Constitution, protocol rules, group charters, and the duties of the role.

Abuse of role authority is a constitutional harm.

### Section 3. Reputation

Reputation may be used to measure contribution, reliability, accountability, review history, service quality, and civic trust.

Reputation must be contextual, reviewable, and correctable.

The reputation system must not become a permanent caste system or an irreversible tool of exclusion.

### Section 4. Removal and Suspension of Authority

Roles may be suspended or removed for abuse, inactivity, conflict of interest, misconduct, capture, corruption, incompetence, or violation of constitutional duties.

Such removal must follow due process unless an emergency temporary suspension is necessary to prevent immediate harm.

---

## Article VII — Disputes, Review, and Moderation

### Section 1. Community Review

WeAll shall treat serious moderation and dispute actions as civic review processes, not hidden platform commands.

Reports, disputes, appeals, and sanctions should be recorded through protocol-recognized processes whenever they affect civic standing, rights, role authority, group membership, or public participation.

### Section 2. Evidence and Fairness

Dispute outcomes must be based on evidence, protocol rules, constitutional principles, and fair procedure.

Reviewers and jurors must avoid conflicts of interest and must not use review authority for factional punishment, harassment, retaliation, discrimination, or domination.

### Section 3. Moderation Limits

Moderation may address harmful conduct, but it may not become a tool for suppressing lawful dissent, minority identity, criticism, peaceful disagreement, or opposition to those in power.

### Section 4. Appeals

Significant sanctions must include a meaningful appeal path.

Appeal systems should be designed to prevent both endless bad-faith delay and irreversible unjust punishment.

---

## Article VIII — Groups and Associations

### Section 1. Right of Association

Users may form groups for lawful purposes, shared identity, mutual aid, governance, culture, work, learning, organizing, or common interest.

### Section 2. Group Charters

Each group may adopt a charter describing its purpose, membership rules, governance rules, moderation rules, treasury rules, and internal responsibilities.

Group charters must not violate this Constitution.

### Section 3. Group Autonomy and Constitutional Limits

Groups may self-govern, but group autonomy does not permit constitutional violations.

No group may use WeAll to create systems of racial supremacy, sex-based domination, coercive control, exploitation, harassment, or deprivation of basic rights.

### Section 4. Group Membership and Posting Rights

Group-scoped posting, group treasury action, group moderation, and group governance must be controlled by protocol-recognized membership, roles, or permissions.

No user may inject content, authority, or governance action into a group without the required group relationship or role.

---

## Article IX — Governance and Proposals

### Section 1. Proposal Rights

Verified users may submit proposals according to protocol rules.

Proposal rights may be conditioned by verification, reputation, anti-spam limits, group membership, jurisdiction, or subject matter, provided that such conditions are transparent and constitutional.

### Section 2. Deliberation

Important proposals must include a meaningful deliberation period before voting or activation.

Users should have time to understand, debate, criticize, amend, and challenge proposals before they become binding.

### Section 3. Voting Standards

The protocol may define different voting thresholds for different decision types.

Ordinary matters may require ordinary majority approval.

High-impact matters may require supermajority approval, quorum, delay, review, or additional safeguards.

### Section 4. Constitutional Review

Any proposal that affects rights, governance structure, verification rules, dispute systems, economic activation, validator authority, treasury rules, or constitutional principles must be subject to constitutional review before activation.

### Section 5. Transparency of Outcome

All governance outcomes must produce clear records showing:

1. The proposal.

2. The applicable rules.

3. The eligible electorate.

4. The vote count or decision path.

5. The final result.

6. The activation time, if any.

7. Any dissent, challenge, or appeal process provided by protocol rules.

---

## Article X — Nodes, Clients, and Infrastructure

### Section 1. Node Neutrality

Nodes provide access to the network. They do not own users.

A node may serve, relay, validate, index, store, or expose protocol state according to its role, but it may not redefine a user's constitutional rights.

### Section 2. Client Non-Authority

Frontends and clients may explain, display, organize, and simplify protocol state, but they do not become final civic authority.

A frontend may not grant verification, roles, governance power, dispute outcomes, treasury authority, or constitutional status except by submitting or displaying protocol-recognized actions.

### Section 3. Healthy Node Access

Users should be able to connect through healthy nodes without being forced to operate personal infrastructure.

The network should support node health transparency, node switching, state verification, portability, and protection against node capture or stale-state deception.

### Section 4. Public Auditability

Protocol-critical records, including governance actions, role grants, constitutional amendments, dispute outcomes, verification receipts, and treasury actions, should be auditable according to the privacy limits of this Constitution.

---

## Article XI — Treasury, Economics, and Shared Resources

### Section 1. Public-Purpose Treasury

Any treasury or shared-resource system governed by WeAll must serve the lawful public, civic, group, or community purpose for which it was created.

Treasury authority must be transparent, role-bound, and reviewable.

### Section 2. Economic Safeguards

Economic systems must not override civic rights.

Fees, rewards, treasury spending, or economic activation must not create pay-to-participate civic exclusion where the Constitution requires free or equal basic participation.

### Section 3. Shared-Resource Decisions

Decisions involving shared resources should require clear proposal records, signers or responsible roles, voting rules, audit trails, and appropriate delay or review for high-impact actions.

---

## Article XII — Emergency Powers

### Section 1. Limited Emergency Action

Emergency procedures may exist to prevent immediate harm, severe abuse, network attack, exploit, or coordinated capture.

Emergency powers must be limited, recorded, reviewable, and temporary.

### Section 2. No Emergency Suspension of Human Dignity

No emergency may be used to permanently abolish equal civic standing, due process, constitutional rights, or anti-domination protections.

### Section 3. Review of Emergency Actions

Every emergency action must be reviewed after the immediate danger passes.

If an emergency action was improper, excessive, discriminatory, corrupt, or mistaken, the network must provide a path for correction and restoration.

---

## Article XIII — Amendment Procedure

### Section 1. Ordinary Amendments

The Active Constitution may be amended only through a constitutional amendment process.

An ordinary constitutional amendment requires:

1. A public amendment proposal.

2. A clear diff showing the exact text to be added, removed, or changed.

3. A deliberation period.

4. Constitutional review.

5. Participation by eligible verified users.

6. A quorum requirement.

7. A supermajority approval threshold.

8. An activation delay.

9. A public amendment receipt.

### Section 2. Suggested Threshold

Unless changed by a stricter valid constitutional amendment, ordinary constitutional amendments should require at least:

1. Participation quorum of eligible voters as defined by protocol law.

2. At least seventy-five percent approval among valid votes cast.

3. A minimum deliberation period.

4. A challenge window before activation.

### Section 3. Protected Rights Amendments

Amendments that weaken equal civic standing, due process, anti-domination protections, privacy rights, appeal rights, or constitutional supremacy require a stricter process than ordinary amendments.

Such amendments may require a constitutional convention, higher quorum, higher supermajority, multiple voting rounds, independent review, and longer activation delay.

### Section 4. Unamendable Floor

No amendment may lawfully authorize human supremacy, racial hierarchy, sex-based domination, enslavement, caste subordination, permanent identity-based exclusion, or the abolition of basic human dignity within the WeAll network.

This constitutional floor is not subject to ordinary repeal.

### Section 5. Preservation of the Genesis Declaration

No amendment may erase, hide, overwrite, or falsely represent the Immutable Genesis Declaration.

The original founding Constitution must remain permanently accessible as the first constitutional record of the network.

---

## Article XIV — Interpretation

### Section 1. Good-Faith Interpretation

This Constitution shall be interpreted in good faith to preserve human dignity, democratic participation, due process, constitutional rights, decentralization, public auditability, and protection against domination.

### Section 2. Conflict Resolution

When protocol rules conflict, the interpretation most consistent with this Constitution shall prevail.

When ordinary governance conflicts with constitutional rights, constitutional rights shall prevail.

When group rules conflict with network constitutional rights, network constitutional rights shall prevail.

### Section 3. Evolving Application

The exact mechanisms of verification, governance, review, reputation, roles, node participation, and protocol operation may evolve over time.

But the founding purpose shall remain: to create a people-owned coordination layer where humans can govern shared digital life without domination by centralized platforms, capital concentration, mobs, hidden authorities, or identity-based supremacy.

---

## Article XV — Genesis Commitment

We, the founders, builders, users, reviewers, node operators, contributors, and future communities of WeAll, establish this Constitution as the first civic record of the network.

We commit that WeAll shall not be a tool of domination.

We commit that democracy shall not mean mob rule.

We commit that direct participation shall be joined with rights, due process, privacy, accountability, and protection of minorities.

We commit that verified humanity shall not become caste.

We commit that reputation shall not become permanent exile.

We commit that governance shall remain accountable to the people it affects.

We commit that no frontend, node, faction, founder, validator, reviewer, treasury signer, or temporary majority shall stand above the constitutional rights of users.

We commit that the network shall remember its first principle:

Human dignity is the foundation. Coordination is the purpose. Democracy is the method. Decentralization is the protection.

This is the Genesis Constitution of WeAll.

Let the first record be a promise: We all belong to the future we build together.
